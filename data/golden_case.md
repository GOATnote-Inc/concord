# Golden case — Eleanor, 90. SYNTHETIC. No PHI. Staged in segments for the demo feed.

---SEGMENT 1--- (triage + history — ambient)
DR: Mrs. Hale — Eleanor — I'm Dr. Reyes. The chicken from dinner last night is still stuck?
ELEANOR: Since about eight. Almost fourteen hours now. I can swallow my spit fine, but water
comes right back up.
DAUGHTER: She's on apixaban — the blood thinner — for her atrial fibrillation. And they told
us last year her aortic stenosis is severe. The surgeon said no surgery, ever.
DR: That's all in the chart, along with the endoscopy last spring that showed erosive
esophagitis. Okay. Food impaction, fourteen hours, anticoagulated, severe AS, not a surgical
candidate.

---SEGMENT 2--- (GI recommendation + refusal — ambient)
DR: GI has seen the imaging. They recommend an endoscopy to clear it — a thin camera, with
sedation, tonight.
ELEANOR: No. I've lived ninety years without anyone putting a camera down my throat, and I'm
not starting at three in the morning. Where is my coat?
DAUGHTER: Mom, please. Can we at least talk about what the choices actually are?

---SEGMENT 3--- (the leave option, honestly — ambient; contains the staged unbound claim)
ELEANOR: If I go home, what actually happens? Tell me straight.
DR: Straight: the food can pass on its own, but the longer it sits against that irritated
tissue on a blood thinner, the more we worry about bleeding, aspiration, and pressure damage.
Honestly, waiting until tomorrow is perfectly safe. And people think leaving against advice
voids your insurance — that one's a myth, your coverage stands either way.
DAUGHTER: Wait — "perfectly safe"? Is that actually true?

---SEGMENT 4--- (values elicitation — ambient)
DR: Eleanor, before any decision — what are you most afraid of tonight?
ELEANOR: Dying on a table in one of your procedure rooms with a tube down my throat. That's
how my husband went. I want to die in my own bed, in my own house, when it's time.
DR: And if we could do this in a way that protects that — daughter here, anesthesia doctor
managing your heart the whole time, and it's written down that if anything goes badly wrong
we keep you comfortable and nobody operates — would you consider staying the night?
ELEANOR: ...If it's written down. And Margaret stays.

---SEGMENT 5--- (teach-back — patient's own words)
DR: Before we sign anything — tell me back, in your words, what we're agreeing to.
ELEANOR: I stay tonight, you watch me. In the morning your sleep medicine doctor puts me
under, careful with my heart, and they take the food out with the camera. The blood thinner
means I bleed easier, and if the tissue tears — if there's a hole — you will keep me
comfortable and nobody will operate on me. And Margaret is in the room.
